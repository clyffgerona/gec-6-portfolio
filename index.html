02 — Course Experience

When Logic Learned to Paint

“

GEC 6 didn't just teach me what art is — it taught me that everything I build as a developer is already art, whether I treat it that way or not.

— Clifford Niño F. Gerona

A Programmer's First Brush Stroke

Coming into GEC 6 as a second-year BSIT student, I carried the mindset of a programmer: everything had to be logical, structured, and purposeful. Art felt abstract and subjective — the opposite of the systems I was used to building. But the course dismantled that rigid wall. My first encounter with the visual elements — line, shape, color, value, texture, and space — felt eerily familiar. These weren't just art concepts; they were the very vocabulary of every interface I had ever coded. A horizontal line wasn't just a design choice; it was a separator element with visual weight and intent.

Composition & the Grid System

The lesson on composition — the rule of thirds, balance, and visual hierarchy — was the moment everything clicked. I had been using CSS Grid and Flexbox for months without understanding why certain layouts felt right. Art theory gave me the language: a centered, symmetrical layout communicates stability and formality; an asymmetric grid suggests dynamism and modernity. Suddenly, my design decisions weren't arbitrary. I was making conscious, intentional choices backed by centuries of visual art philosophy.

Color Theory → UI/UX Superpower

Color theory was arguably the most transformative part of the course for my development work. Learning about hue, saturation, value, and color harmony (complementary, analogous, triadic) directly upgraded my ability to design interfaces that are not only functional but emotionally resonant. I learned that a deep charcoal background with a vibrant cyan accent isn't just "dark mode" — it's a deliberate emotional statement: technical precision, forward-looking energy, and creative tension. Every palette I now choose tells a story before a single line of text is read.

The Bigger Picture: Art as Empathy

Perhaps the most profound lesson was understanding art as a form of empathy — an attempt to communicate feelings, ideas, and experiences across time and culture. As a future IT professional, I will build systems that millions of people interact with. GEC 6 reminded me that behind every screen, every button, every pixel, there is a human being with emotions and expectations. Designing with that awareness — with artistic sensitivity and technical precision — is the difference between software that merely functions and software that genuinely connects.

A Letter of Gratitude

JA

Ma'am Jhonna D. Alcantara

GEC 6: Visual Arts Camarines Norte State College

To Ma'am Jhonna D. Alcantara,

Writing this feedback letter is, in itself, an exercise in everything GEC 6 taught me: finding the right words, arranging them with intention, and ensuring that the structure serves the emotion. So with that lens, allow me to offer my deepest and most sincere gratitude.

When I first enrolled in GEC 6: Visual Arts, I will be honest — I was uncertain about its relevance to my program. I am a BSIT student. I think in loops and conditionals, in functions and frameworks. Art, I thought, was a different world, spoken in a different language. You showed me, patiently and systematically, that this was never true.

What struck me most from the very beginning was the clarity and fairness of your rubrics. In a subject that could easily feel subjective, you created a structure where effort, growth, and creativity were measurable and rewarded equally. The video report rubric, for instance, wasn't just a grading tool — it was a masterclass in expectation-setting. It showed me what excellent communication looks like before I even attempted it, which is precisely how the best software requirements documents should work.

Your teaching style made complex art theory approachable without ever making it feel simplified. You connected classical concepts — the visual elements, composition, color harmony — to contexts that made sense to us as 21st-century students. The way you structured discussions, welcomed questions, and provided consistent feedback created a classroom environment where it was genuinely safe to experiment and even to fail beautifully.

The activities you designed were not busywork. Each one was a carefully placed stepping stone that built upon the last. I entered this course knowing how to write code. I leave it knowing how to design — truly design — with intention, empathy, and an eye trained to see balance, hierarchy, and beauty where I once saw only layout.

As I continue my journey in Information Technology, I carry with me the knowledge that the best interfaces are not just functional — they are felt. That understanding is your gift to me, Ma'am. For that, I am profoundly grateful.

With deep respect and sincere appreciation,

Clifford Niño F. Gerona BSIT 2A — GEC 6 Student
